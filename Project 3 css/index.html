What is Treaty 1?

Treaty 1 was the first of the numbered treaties signed in Canada. It was an agreement between the government of Canada and various First Nations. The treaty was signed on August 3, 1871, at Lower Fort Garry, which is near present-day Winnipeg, Manitoba.

The Canadian government was represented by officials, including the Lieutenant Governor of Manitoba, Adams G. Archibald. First Nation leaders for the Anishinaabe (Ojibwe) and the Cree included Peguis, Sandy Bay, Brokenhead, and Long Plain First Nation.

The Canadian government wanted to open up the land for settlement and the construction of railways. First Nations leaders signed the treaty to protect their land, receive annual payments, and obtain farming tools and other essentials.

The government promised land of 160 acres per family of five, annual payments, and tools to work on farms. The First Nations did not cede their ownership but agreed to share the land, believing they would coexist peacefully with settlers.

Not all promises of the treaty were kept. Resources and support promised to the First Nations were often not received. Many misunderstandings arose due to language differences and varied interpretations of the treaty's meaning.

Treaty 1 remains relevant today, symbolizing the bond shared by the Canadian government and First Nations people. Many continue to advocate for treaty rights and the rectification of past wrongs.

Understanding Treaty 1 helps us learn about Indigenous history and culture, highlighting the importance of cooperation and respect for agreements.

Did You Know? Treaty 1 is often referred to as the Selkirk Treaty because it encompasses the area of the Selkirk Settlement.

Key Terms of Treaty 1

  • Land Surrender: Indigenous signatories agreed to surrender a portion of their traditional lands in exchange for certain rights and benefits.
  • Reserve Creation: The treaty provided for the establishment of reserves for the Indigenous peoples, ensuring they had designated areas for their communities.
  • Financial Compensation: Annuities were promised to the Indigenous signatories, providing them with regular payments as part of the agreement.
  • Right to Hunt and Fish: The treaty recognized the right of Indigenous peoples to continue their traditional hunting, fishing, and gathering practices on their lands.
  • Education and Assistance: The Crown committed to providing education and assistance to the Indigenous communities as part of the treaty obligations.

Impact

The signing of Treaty 1 marked the beginning of a series of treaties that would significantly alter the relationship between Indigenous peoples and the Canadian government. While it was intended to promote peace, the treaty has been criticized for its implementation and the subsequent marginalization of Indigenous communities.
